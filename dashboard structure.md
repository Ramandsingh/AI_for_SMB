Landing Page — "The AI Adoption Dashboard for Specialist Wholesale"
Purpose: Orient the user. Show them the whole journey at a glance and let them jump to the page most relevant to where they are.
Sections:
1. Welcome & How to Use This Dashboard
A short framing statement: "AI isn't a destination, it's a staircase. This dashboard helps you climb deliberately." One paragraph each on who it's for (business leaders in wholesale, not technical teams), what it delivers (a structured path from awareness to operational value), and how to navigate (start with self-assessment, or jump to a section).
2. The AI Journey at a Glance
A single visual — the five-stage maturity curve (Assistant → Accelerator → Automator → Operator → Operating Model) with indicative timelines, investment ranges, and value types at each stage. This is the hero graphic of the whole dashboard.
3. Where Are You Today? (Self-Assessment Entry Point)
A short diagnostic (10–15 questions) that places the business at a current maturity stage and recommends which pages to focus on. This is what makes it a dashboard and not a document — it routes the user.
4. Quick Navigation Tiles
Seven tiles linking to the subordinate pages, each with a one-line description so the user can self-route.
5. Evidence Base & Credibility Strip
A footer-style strip naming the frameworks the dashboard draws from (Gartner, MIT CISR, MITRE) and key statistics (productivity gains, ROI benchmarks, failure rates) with sources. Signals rigour without getting in the way.

A few design principles for the dashboard itself
A couple of things worth stating, because they'll shape how this actually gets built:
The self-assessment entry point is what turns this from a document into a dashboard. Without it, users wander. With it, the dashboard becomes genuinely navigational — "you're at Stage 2, here's what matters for you right now." Invest in making that diagnostic actually good. Ten questions, well-designed, that route users to the three or four sections most relevant to them.
The ROI calculator is the page that creates stickiness. If users can model their own numbers, they come back. If they can't, it's a one-read document. Make the calculator as usable as possible, even if it means simpler math — a CFO would rather use a simple calculator that's clearly theirs than a sophisticated one they don't trust.
The role impact cards are the most shared artefact. Design them to be self-contained and printable. A finance manager will screenshot their card. A warehouse manager will email theirs to their team. That's the dashboard spreading inside the client organisation, which is exactly what you want.
The evidence base should be quietly present, not loud. A thin strip on the landing page, consistent citation style on claims, a dedicated "sources" subsection on each page where statistics appear. Sophisticated buyers will check; unsophisticated buyers just need to feel it's rigorous.
Consider two navigation modes: journey and reference. Journey mode walks a user from landing page through pages 1–7 in order. Reference mode lets them jump to specific pages or sections. Both should be obvious from the landing page. First-time users usually want journey; returning users want reference.
